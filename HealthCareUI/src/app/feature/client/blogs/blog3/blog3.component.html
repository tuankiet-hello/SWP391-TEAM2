How to Talk About Sex With Your Teen

Source: Mayo Clinic

Sex education is offered in many schools, but don't count on classroom instruction alone. Sex education needs to happen at home too. Here's help talking to your teen about sex.

Sex education basics may be covered in health class. But teens might not hear — or understand — everything they need to know to make tough choices about sex. That's where you come in.

It can be awkward, but sex education is a parent's job. By connecting with your teen early and often, you can set the stage for a lifetime of healthy sexuality.

Sex might be hard to talk about. But it's even harder to avoid. Sex seems to be everywhere — news, entertainment, social media, advertising. But you can use that to get the talk going and keep it going.

  1. Start the Conversation Early and Keep It Going

    Use everyday opportunities — news stories, TV shows, social media — to bring up topics about sex, relationships, and values. Don't wait for "the talk"; make it a series of ongoing, honest conversations.

  2. Cover the Basics and Beyond

    Sex education for teens should include not only the facts about reproduction and STIs, but also topics like abstinence, consent, date rape, gender identity, sexual orientation, and healthy relationships. Be ready for tough questions.

  3. Discuss Consent and Boundaries

    Make it clear that "no" always means no. Sex should never be pressured or forced. Any form of forced sex is rape, whether it's done by a stranger or someone your teen knows. Talk about the effects of alcohol and drugs on decision-making and consent.

  4. Support Teens Questioning Gender or Sexual Orientation

    Many teens wonder about their sexual orientation, gender identity, or expression. Let your teen know that feelings may change over time, and that's okay. A negative reaction can have serious consequences for LGBTQ youth. Family acceptance is protective.

    Above all, let your teen know your love is unconditional. Praise them for sharing their feelings and listen more than you speak.

  5. Address Dating Violence and Healthy Relationships

    About 1 in 12 teens has experienced physical or sexual dating violence. Share the facts and warning signs with your teen. Teens in abusive relationships have higher risks of poor grades, binge drinking, and suicide attempts. Model healthy relationships and teach respect, boundaries, and understanding right from wrong.

  6. If Your Teen Is Sexually Active

    If your teen is sexually active, keep the conversation open and honest. Remind them that sex and its responsibilities are serious . Encourage regular checkups, private conversations with healthcare providers, and learning about contraception and safe sex.

    Discuss the importance of routine HPV vaccination, which protects against genital warts and several cancers. The vaccine is usually given between ages 9 and 26, but may be available to older people as well.

  7. Your Guidance Matters

    Be honest and speak from the heart. Even if your teen seems uninterested, say it anyway — they are probably listening. Your support is key to helping your teen become a sexually responsible adult.

Sex education is an ongoing process. By talking openly, listening, and supporting your teen, you help them make healthy, informed choices about their sexuality and relationships.
